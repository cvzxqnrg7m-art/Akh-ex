Early Christian Testimony: The Crucifixion Question

First-century evidence of Christians who denied Jesus was crucified

Ignatius of Antioch: First-Century Bishop

Ignatius, known as Saint Ignatius of Antioch, was a first-century Christian bishop martyred around 108 AD. Born in the early 30s AD, his life overlapped with Jesus's disciples, including Jesus's brother James who headed the Jerusalem church.

Letter to the Magnesians, Section 9
"We have seen how former adherents of the ancient customs have since attained to a new hope... The day when life first dawned for us thanks to him and his death - that death, though some deny it, is the very mystery which has moved us to become believers."
Born: Early 30s AD - overlapped with Jesus's contemporaries
Position: Second or third bishop of Antioch after Peter
Fate: Martyred in Rome, thrown to lions around 108 AD

First-Century Christians Who Denied Crucifixion

Ignatius's testimony reveals a significant fact: in the first century, there were Christians who denied that Jesus died on the cross. This belief, later found in Islam, was present among early Christian communities.

Key Phrase: "though some deny it" - explicit acknowledgment of dissenters
Historical Context: This is not a later Islamic invention but first-century Christian testimony
Islamic Connection: Quranic teaching aligns with these early Christian beliefs
Significance of the Testimony
"This is remarkable first-century testimony from a prominent Christian about other Christians who did not agree with Ignatius. While Ignatius condemns these people, their mere existence is historically significant evidence."

The Great Irony: Rejecting Jesus's Religion

Ignatius makes a startling statement that represents one of history's great ironies: Christianity's rejection of the very religion practiced by Jesus and his immediate followers.

Letter to the Magnesians, Section 10
"To profess Jesus Christ while continuing to follow Jewish customs is an absurdity. The Christian faith does not look to Judaism, but Judaism looks to Christianity."

The Irony: Jesus himself, according to the Gospels, taught obedience to Jewish law:

Matthew 23:2-3: "The scribes and Pharisees sit on Moses' seat; therefore, do whatever they teach you and follow it."
Matthew 23:23: "You tithe mint, dill, and cumin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice, mercy, and faith. It is these you ought to have practiced without neglecting the others."
Matthew 5:17: "Do not think that I have come to abolish the law... not one letter, not one stroke of a letter, will pass from the law."

James: Head of the Jerusalem Church

Contrary to Catholic claims about Peter, the New Testament and early sources indicate James (Jesus's brother) was the head of the Jerusalem church and a practicing Jew.

Position: Head of Jerusalem church, appointed by apostles including Peter
Practice: Known as "James the Just" - zealous for Jewish law
Timeline: Led church until temple destruction (70 AD)
Acts 21:20
"You see, brother, how many thousands of believers there are among the Jews, and they are all zealous for the law."

Historical Testimony: Jewish historian Josephus and early Christian sources describe James as famous among Jews for his zeal for Jewish law.

Timeline: The Great Historical Irony

c. 30 AD

Jesus's Ministry: Teaches obedience to Jewish law, tells followers to obey those who "sit on Moses' seat" (Matthew 23:2-3).

30-60 AD

Jerusalem Church: Led by James (Jesus's brother). Thousands of Jewish believers "zealous for the law" (Acts 21:20). James known for Torah observance.

c. 108 AD

Ignatius of Antioch: Declares: "To profess Jesus Christ while continuing to follow Jewish customs is an absurdity." First recorded use of word "Christianity."

The Irony

The Contradiction: Emerging Gentile Christianity rejects the very religion practiced by Jesus, his brother James, and the Jerusalem church. Christianity disowns its parent faith.

The Historical Significance

"One of the great ironies of history: the later emergent Christianity which claims Jesus as its founder rejected the very religion of its founder. We see this rejection explicitly and very early in Christian history."
