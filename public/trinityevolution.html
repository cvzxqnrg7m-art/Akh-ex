Pattern of Textual Corruption

A clear pattern emerges: scribes altered biblical texts to support developing Trinitarian theology. These changes systematically enhance Jesus's divinity and add explicit Trinitarian formulations not found in earliest manuscripts.

Historical Context: Alterations correspond with periods of intense Trinitarian debate (4th-6th centuries).
Critical Question: If Trinity were clearly biblical, why were textual alterations necessary?
Impact: KJV and other translations based on altered texts perpetuate these corruptions.

Bart Ehrman on Trinity Development

Textual Alterations

"Scribes didn't just translate the text, they interpreted it. And sometimes their interpretations became the text. The textual variants show scribes trying to put the Trinity into the Bible."
Analysis: Systematic changes made to support developing theology

Historical Evolution

"The Trinity is not in the Bible. It developed over centuries. If the doctrine were clearly biblical, why would it take 300 years of debate and councils to define it?"
Analysis: Doctrine evolved through political and theological conflicts

Theological Paradox

The Trinity: 300-Year Evolution

1st Century

Jewish Monotheism

Earliest Christians maintained strict Jewish monotheism. Jesus as Messiah, not God. Prayer directed to God through Jesus.

Key Evidence: Shema recited, no Trinitarian formulas
2nd Century

Logos Theology

Greek philosophical concepts introduced. Theophilus coins "trias." Jesus as God's Word/Wisdom, subordinate to Father.

Justin Martyr: "We worship the Word who came from Him... second after God."
3rd Century

Tertullian's "Trinity"

Tertullian coins Latin "trinitas." Maintains subordination: Son subordinate to Father. Debates Modalism.

Tertullian: "The Son is not the Father, but God from God."
4th Century

Arian Controversy & Nicaea

Arius: "There was when he was not." Council of Nicaea (325): adopts non-biblical "homoousios." Political enforcement begins.

Constantine: "Division in the Church is worse than war."
5th Century

Augustine's Refinement

Augustine writes "On the Trinity." Develops psychological analogy: memory (Father), understanding (Son), will (Spirit).

Augustine: "The Trinity is in humanity: mind, knowledge, love."
6th Century+

Consolidation & Paradox

Athanasian Creed formalizes paradoxical formulas. "One God in Trinity, Trinity in unity." Mystery embraced as virtue.

Athanasian Creed: "Three persons, one substance; neither confusing nor dividing."
